Apollo 11 was the American spaceflight mission that achieved the first crewed landing on the Moon. The mission was part of NASA's Apollo program, which was developed during the Cold War space race between the United States and the Soviet Union. Apollo 11 launched from Kennedy Space Center in Florida on July 16, 1969, aboard a Saturn V rocket. The three astronauts on the mission were Neil Armstrong, Buzz Aldrin, and Michael Collins. Collins remained in lunar orbit aboard the command module Columbia while Armstrong and Aldrin traveled to the surface in the lunar module Eagle.

On July 20, 1969, Eagle landed in the Sea of Tranquility after Armstrong manually guided the spacecraft away from a hazardous area. Armstrong became the first person to step onto the Moon, followed by Aldrin. During their moonwalk the astronauts collected rock and soil samples, deployed scientific instruments, and took photographs. They also planted an American flag and left behind a plaque stating that they came in peace for all mankind. The televised landing became one of the most widely watched events in history.

Apollo 11 demonstrated that the United States could carry out a complex mission involving launch, lunar landing, surface operations, rendezvous, and safe return to Earth. After completing their work on the Moon, Armstrong and Aldrin rejoined Collins in orbit, and the crew began the journey home. They splashed down in the Pacific Ocean on July 24, 1969, where they were recovered by the aircraft carrier USS Hornet. The mission had scientific value, but it also had major political and symbolic importance because it showed a dramatic technological achievement at a tense moment in global history.
