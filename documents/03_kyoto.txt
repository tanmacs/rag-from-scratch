Kyoto is a historic city in Japan that served as the country's imperial capital for more than a thousand years. It was established in 794 under the name Heian-kyo, and for centuries it functioned as the political and cultural center of the imperial court. Although Tokyo later became the national capital, Kyoto retained enormous symbolic importance because it preserved rituals, architecture, and artistic traditions tied to earlier periods of Japanese history. The city is located in a basin in the Kansai region and is surrounded by mountains, a setting that influenced both its climate and urban development.

Kyoto is known for its dense concentration of temples, shrines, gardens, and traditional wooden neighborhoods. Famous sites include Kiyomizu-dera, Kinkaku-ji, and Fushimi Inari Taisha, each reflecting different aspects of religious practice and architectural style. Seasonal change plays a major role in the city's identity. Cherry blossoms in spring and red maple leaves in autumn attract many visitors, while summer festivals such as Gion Matsuri connect contemporary life to long-standing civic traditions. Craft production also remains important, including textiles, ceramics, lacquerware, and tea-related goods.

Modern Kyoto is not simply a museum city. It is also home to universities, research institutions, and technology companies, which means old and new coexist closely. Narrow lanes with machiya townhouses can stand only a short distance from rail lines and modern commercial districts. During the Second World War Kyoto was notably spared from the atomic bombings that destroyed Hiroshima and Nagasaki, a fact that helped preserve much of its historical built environment. Because of that continuity, the city offers an unusually rich record of Japanese urban, religious, and artistic history across many centuries.
