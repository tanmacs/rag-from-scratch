Photosynthesis is the biological process that allows plants, algae, and some bacteria to convert light energy into chemical energy. In green plants the process takes place mainly inside chloroplasts, which contain the pigment chlorophyll. Chlorophyll absorbs portions of the visible light spectrum, especially red and blue wavelengths, and helps drive the reactions that build energy-rich molecules. The overall result is that carbon dioxide from the air and water from the environment are used to make glucose, while oxygen is released as a by-product.

Scientists often describe photosynthesis in two linked stages. The first stage is the light-dependent reaction, which occurs in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast. In this stage absorbed light energy is used to split water molecules, releasing oxygen and generating ATP and NADPH. These molecules temporarily store energy that the plant can use in later reactions. The second stage is the Calvin cycle, which takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast. In the Calvin cycle the plant uses ATP and NADPH to fix carbon dioxide into organic molecules that eventually contribute to glucose formation.

Photosynthesis matters because it supports nearly all food webs on Earth. Plants use the glucose they produce to grow roots, stems, leaves, flowers, and seeds, while animals obtain energy by consuming plants or other animals that ate plants. The oxygen released during photosynthesis also helped transform Earth's atmosphere over long timescales, making aerobic life possible. Environmental conditions such as light intensity, temperature, water availability, and carbon dioxide concentration all influence the rate of photosynthesis. When any of these factors becomes limiting, the process slows down, which can reduce growth and crop yield.
