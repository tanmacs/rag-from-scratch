Ada Lovelace was an English mathematician and writer who is often remembered for her work on Charles Babbage's proposed Analytical Engine. She was born Augusta Ada Byron in London on December 10, 1815, the daughter of the poet Lord Byron and Annabella Milbanke. Her mother encouraged the study of mathematics and logic, partly because she wanted Ada to avoid the emotional extremes associated with Byron. Ada was educated by private tutors, and she developed a strong interest in machines, patterns, and formal reasoning while still young.

Lovelace met Charles Babbage in 1833 and became fascinated by his Difference Engine and his more ambitious Analytical Engine design. The Analytical Engine was never completed, but it introduced many ideas that later appeared in modern computers. It would have used punched cards for input, a memory store for data, and a mill for processing calculations. In 1843 Lovelace translated an article about the engine by the Italian engineer Luigi Menabrea. She added a set of extensive notes that became much longer than the original article itself.

One of those notes described a step-by-step method for using the machine to calculate Bernoulli numbers. Because that note outlined a process that can be interpreted as a computer program, Lovelace is frequently called the first computer programmer. Her importance is not limited to that label. She also argued that a general-purpose machine could manipulate symbols, not only numbers, if those symbols could be represented according to rules. That insight anticipated later ideas about computers handling music, text, and images. Ada Lovelace died on November 27, 1852, at the age of thirty-six, but her writings remain central to the history of computing.
