Plate tectonics is the scientific theory that explains the large-scale movement of Earth's lithosphere. According to the theory, the outer shell of the Earth is broken into rigid plates that move slowly over the softer asthenosphere beneath them. This movement is driven by processes such as mantle convection, slab pull, and ridge push. Although the plates shift only a few centimeters each year, their motion over millions of years reshapes oceans, continents, and mountain belts.

There are several kinds of plate boundaries, and each produces characteristic geological effects. At divergent boundaries, plates move apart and new crust forms, as seen along mid-ocean ridges. At convergent boundaries, plates move toward one another. One plate may sink beneath another in a process called subduction, which can generate deep ocean trenches, volcanic arcs, and powerful earthquakes. When continental plates collide, they can crumple and uplift large mountain ranges such as the Himalayas. At transform boundaries, plates slide past one another horizontally, producing faults like the San Andreas Fault in California.

Plate tectonics helps explain why earthquakes and volcanoes are concentrated in specific zones rather than distributed randomly. It also clarifies how fossils and rock formations on widely separated continents can show striking similarities. Before plate tectonics was widely accepted, scientists debated continental drift and seafloor spreading as separate ideas. The modern theory unified those observations into a single framework in the mid-twentieth century. Today plate tectonics is one of the foundational concepts of Earth science because it links deep planetary processes with the landscapes and hazards observed at the surface.
